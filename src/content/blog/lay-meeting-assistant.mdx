Lay came from a very ordinary kind of exhaustion.

I was spending too much time in meetings.

Not one dramatic meeting. Not one obviously broken process. Just the slow accumulation of calls, updates, planning sessions, follow-ups, demos, and context switches. By the end of the day, I would remember that something important had been said, but not always where. A decision was in one call. A risk was mentioned in another. A follow-up lived in my notes, unless it lived in chat, unless I forgot to write it down because I was presenting.

The problem was not that I needed a better notes app.

The problem was that meetings create a strange private workspace. You need notes while sharing your screen. You need reminders while someone else is talking. You need to ask a quick question about the meeting without pasting half the meeting into another tool. You need to review what just happened while the next conversation is already starting.

I wanted a small layer for that.

Private, fast, and aware of the meeting.

## Notes That Do Not Become A Performance

Most meeting tools assume the notes are either private documents you manage later or collaborative artifacts everyone can see.

I wanted something more immediate.

A place to write rough notes while the meeting is happening. A place that can sit on top of everything else. A place that does not appear in screen sharing. A place that is allowed to be messy because it is helping me think, not trying to become the final artifact.

That is why Lay is an always-on-top desktop app instead of a web page. It opens as a small frameless window, stays at the top-right, supports opacity hotkeys, and keeps notes in `~/.lay/notes.md`.

The window behavior matters because the use case is interruption-heavy. You do not want to hunt for the app while presenting. You do not want to reorganize your screen. You want to toggle it, jot the thing down, and get back to the conversation.

## The Assistant Needed The Meeting, Not Just A Prompt

The next step was obvious after using plain notes for a while: sometimes I did not need to write more. I needed to ask the meeting a question.

What did they decide?

What are the open follow-ups?

What did I promise to check?

What context should I carry into the next call?

A generic chat box cannot answer those unless you manually feed it the context. Lay tries to make the meeting itself part of the assistant's working memory.

The app has notes, chat, transcription, voice, and settings as separate tabs. Chat stays mounted so the conversation survives tab switches. Transcription and voice stay mounted so recording can survive tab switches too. On the backend, Lay builds the assistant system prompt from the current transcript or the live transcript segments when recording is active.

That is the real product shape: not "AI chat in a small window," but a meeting-aware assistant that can use the session transcript when answering.

## Why It Has To Stay Private

There is an awkward social detail in this kind of tool: the assistant is useful precisely because it is not part of the meeting surface.

If I am sharing my screen, I do not want my rough notes, half-formed questions, or private assistant prompts visible to everyone. If I am presenting, I may need a reminder or quick answer without turning the call into a live demo of my coping mechanisms.

So Lay's screen-capture behavior is not decoration. It is the boundary that makes the tool usable.

The same goes for the local files. Notes live under `~/.lay`. Config lives under `~/.lay/config.json`. A gateway can be embedded at build time or overridden by the user with `~/.lay/gateway.json`, so a company can route model calls through its own Chat Completions-compatible endpoint.

The app is small, but the privacy boundary is doing a lot of work.

## What I Was Actually Building

I was not trying to replace calendars, docs, or meeting platforms.

I was trying to survive the fog between them.

Lay is the layer I wanted during the day: rough notes, quick review, transcript-aware questions, voice or transcription when needed, and a private assistant that stays out of the shared screen.

The useful part is not any single feature. It is the combination:

- always there;
- not visible to the meeting;
- close to my notes;
- aware of the transcript;
- fast enough to use while the conversation is still moving.

That is the shape I kept needing between meetings, so that is the shape I built.
